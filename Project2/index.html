Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Debbie Hernandez — dah023@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Access to abortion services should be expanded across U.S. states because less services does not impact the abortion rate.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • +2 Grouped bar chart - Clear side-by-side comparison between lack of access and travel burden.
  • -1 Top 10 States only - Focuses attention on extreme cases; omits national context.
  • +1 Color pallete - Blue = access; red = burden to show immediate emotional contrast.
  • +1 Anotation (Highlighted State) - Adds focus on most affected state.
  • +1 Ordered by % without Clinic - Improves readability and flow, directs attention to extremes.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • +2 Scatter plot for correlation - Shows reationship between clinic and abortion-rate changes.
  • -2 Straight trendline - used on potentially heteroskedastic data to imply a stronger linear relationship.
  • 0 Quadrant shading - Makes directionality instantly clear.
  • -1 Descriptive Quadrant labels - (clinics↓, rates↓ and clinics↑, rates↑) to sell a narrative while ignoring other quadrants.
  • -1 Removing of 0% change states - Reduces noise but hides neutral cases.
  • +1 Red/Green Dot Marks - Universal colors for decline vs grouwth.

Final Reflection

Creating these two visualizations showed me that persuasive data design depends less on numbers and more on framing and selection. The bar chart “Fewer Clinics ≠ Fewer Abortions” was straightforward to build the comparison of access and travel was intuitive, but I quickly saw how data selection, color, order, and annotation could shift tone from neutral to advocacy. The scatterplot “Fewer Clinics, Fewer Abortions!” was a bit tougher. Deciding how to treat neutral states, set axis ranges, and draw the trendline forced me to confront how easily correlation can imply causation.

What I enjoyed the most was realizing that even honest design choices carry persuasive impact and visual influence. Small adjustments in hue, symmetry, or phrasing can change how evidence feels. But what surprised me was how some minor design choices can make me feel deceptive. I now define ethical visualization as aligning visual emphasis with the actual strength of data persuading proportionally rather than distorting. The boundary between “acceptable” and “misleading” lies in intent, removing uncertainty or exaggerating relationships I believe crosses it. Clarifying patterns without hiding complexity does not. Ethical design, for me, means shaping understanding without obscuring truth.
